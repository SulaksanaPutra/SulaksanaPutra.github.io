Twin — In-house Distributor System

Domain

Distribution / Inventory / Sales Operations

Primary responsibility

Centralized previously fragmented, offline distributor workflows into a single cloud-based system, establishing a shared operational backbone for sales, inventory, delivery, back-office processes, and cross-branch reporting across multiple locations.

Scale and constraints

Business-critical system used daily by two distributor companies and all their branches. Built as a legacy Laravel monolith with a large, growing database, limited automated testing, and real-time operational tables. Scaling was constrained to vertical scaling due to architectural and time limitations.

Legacy context

Inherited a large, production-critical codebase with minimal testing, duplicated business logic, and unclear architectural boundaries. Major refactors carried high risk due to the system’s central role in daily operations.

My role

Inherited and maintained the system, led incremental refactoring in high-risk areas, introduced unit testing for critical logic, resolved major tax-calculation issues, and supported rollout to additional branch offices. Shared direct responsibility for production stability within a small core team.

Outcome

Replaced paper-based and branch-isolated processes with a centralized system relied on for daily operations, improving data consistency, reducing manual reconciliation, and enabling real-time visibility across distributor branches.

Twin v2 — Warehouse Management System

Domain

Warehouse Management / Distribution / Logistics

Primary responsibility

Manages warehouse operations and stock movement as a standalone system, replacing the stock management responsibilities of the legacy in-house platform. Designed as a foundation for a future SaaS offering while remaining integrated with existing sales and order systems.

Scale and constraints

Manages warehouse operations and stock movement as a standalone system, replacing the stock management responsibilities of the legacy in-house platform. Designed as a foundation for a future SaaS offering while remaining integrated with existing sales and order systems.

Architecture & design context

Backend designed as a set of independent services with separate databases, implemented in Go for performance, reliability, and cost efficiency. Frontend shared across multiple applications to reduce setup and operational overhead. Architectural decisions balanced general SaaS design against customer-specific requirements inherited from the legacy system.

My role

Designed and owned the backend architecture, including service boundaries, data separation, and integration contracts. Contributed to frontend architecture decisions for the warehouse module. Took primary responsibility for backend stability and correctness in production.

Trade-offs & decisions

Chose Go to improve reliability and reduce infrastructure cost, accepting a slower development pace. Prioritized correctness and stability over rapid feature expansion. Accepted limited modularity in some areas due to customer pressure and tight integration with the legacy system, deferring full SaaS-level generalization.

Outcome

Delivered a more reliable and scalable stock management system with clearer architecture than the legacy platform. Improved correctness of inventory calculations and enabled horizontal scaling, easier backups, and safer data movement across environments.

Twin v2 – Fleet Management System

Domain

Logistics / Delivery Operations

Design intent

Built as a standalone delivery domain when the company separated warehouse and delivery into distinct divisions. The goal was to encapsulate delivery workflows into an independent system, enabling focused development without warehouse-related coupling, while remaining integrable as part of a broader SaaS platform.

Primary responsibility

Manages delivery execution from warehouse cut-off to final transaction settlement, including shipment tracking, driver operations, delivery status, and post-delivery reconciliation.

Scale and constraints

Manages delivery execution from warehouse cut-off to final transaction settlement, including shipment tracking, driver operations, delivery status, and post-delivery reconciliation.

Key complexity

Although simpler than WMS in flow, the system required high transparency. This resulted in complex relational data models and extensive logging of user actions and state changes. A major design challenge was aligning inconsistent legacy business logic—such as unit measurements—into a stable, general-purpose model without breaking daily operations.

Reliability approach

Strong emphasis on integration safety. Failure in inter-system communication (WMS, Sales) would cause cascading business impact, so constant consistency checks and saga-style coordination were implemented to protect transaction settlement.

LAAS – Logistics as a Service Platform

Domain

Customer-facing Logistics Platform (B2B & B2C)

Design intent

Created to support a new business model where warehouse capacity and logistics operations could be rented to external customers while still being used internally. Designed as a customer-facing access layer that exposes logistics capabilities without exposing or coupling to internal operational systems.

Primary responsibility

Acts as a proxy and integration layer between customers and the logistics company. Allows customers to manage stock movements, inventory actions, and logistics requests through a simplified interface, while internally forwarding commands to Twin v2 systems (WMS, Fleet) in a controlled and isolated manner.

Scale and constraints

Supportive rather than business-critical for internal operations. If LAAS fails, internal logistics systems continue operating normally. Designed for easy external access, integration friendliness, and safe isolation from core operational systems.

Key complexity

Reliability and transparency in an asynchronous proxy system. Requests are handled using queues and background jobs, following a fire-and-forget model. This required careful logging, job monitoring, and clear traceability to compensate for the lack of immediate synchronous feedback to users.

Reliability approach

Reliability and transparency in an asynchronous proxy system. Requests are handled using queues and background jobs, following a fire-and-forget model. This required careful logging, job monitoring, and clear traceability to compensate for the lack of immediate synchronous feedback to users.

My role

Sole designer and technical decision-maker for the system. Led development with support from two other developers. Chose architecture, patterns, and trade-offs independently, based on lessons learned from previous systems.

Trade-offs & decisions

Chose Laravel to meet tight delivery timelines and accelerate development, accepting performance and strictness limitations compared to Go. Mitigated these weaknesses by enforcing service-layer boundaries, clear DTO usage, and restricting Laravel’s role to validation and data access, keeping business logic explicit and readable.

Outcome

Enabled a new logistics business model by giving customers real-time access to logistics capabilities through a user-friendly interface. Successfully shielded internal systems from direct customer interaction, reducing risk while increasing integration flexibility.

What I would change today

Improve transparency for asynchronous failures by investing more in user-facing job status and observability tooling. Performance tuning would also be a priority if the system scaled beyond low-to-mid traffic.
